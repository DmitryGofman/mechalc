Snap-Fit Theory, Derivations & Design Ideas

Every equation the calculator uses, derived from first principles, plus the handbook tricks for buying more deflection than a plain beam allows. Sources: BASF Snap-Fit Design Manual and Bayer Snap-Fit Joints for Plastics; the section constants are re-derived here and cross-checked numerically in the calculator at runtime.

1 · Model & symbols

A cantilever snap-fit is treated as an end-loaded Euler–Bernoulli beam: plane sections stay plane, shear deformation is negligible, the root is ideally fixed in a rigid wall, and deflections are small enough to use the undeformed geometry. The polymer is represented by a secant modulus at the working strain (§5). Everything below follows from those assumptions — and fails with them, which is why the calculator holds its verdict outside the envelope (short arms L/t < 10, large deflection y/L > 0.1–0.25).

L arm length (mm) · b root width (mm) · t root thickness (mm) · y undercut = tip deflection (mm) · P transverse tip force (N) · W, W′ insertion / removal force (N) · Es secant modulus (MPa) · I second moment of area (mm⁴) · ε root surface strain (–) · α, α′ entry / return ramp angle · μ friction coefficient · Kt concentration factor · R root fillet radius (mm)

2 · The uniform arm — identical to the classical cantilever

For a rectangular section, the second moment of area and the standard end-loaded cantilever deflection are

I=bt312 y=PL3 3EsI

Substituting I and solving for the force to reach a prescribed deflection y:

P= Esbt3 4L3⁢y k=Py= Esb4 (tL)3

The bending moment peaks at the root, Mmax = P·L, and the surface strain there is ε = M·(t/2)/(Es·I). Substituting P eliminates the modulus entirely:

εmax= 3t⁢y 2L2 =1.50⁢ t⁢yL2
strain is pure geometry — the modulus only sets the force

This is the deepest fact in snap-fit design: in a displacement-controlled event (the hook must travel y, whatever that takes), the strain depends only on the shape. A softer grade doesn't reduce strain — it only makes the click easier. To survive, change geometry: strain falls with L², linearly with thinner t, and not at all with b.

3 · Tapered arms

For a variable section, Castigliano's theorem gives the tip compliance as an integral over the varying stiffness:

y=P ∫0L (L−x)2 EsI(x) dx

With a linear thickness taper t(x) = t·(1 − x/2L) (tip = t/2) or a width taper b(x) = b·(1 − 3x/4L) (tip = b/4), the integral evaluates to the handbook constants. The calculator re-computes this integral numerically on every run and displays the Δ% against the closed form — that's the "independent check" line.

ProfileForceRoot strainPermissible deflection vs uniform
UniformP = Esb(t/L)³y / 4ε = 1.50·ty/L²×1.00
Thickness taper t→t/2P = Esb(t/L)³y / 6.528ε = 0.92·ty/L²×1.63
Width taper b→b/4P = Esb(t/L)³y / 5.136ε = 1.17·ty/L²×1.28

Why tapering helps: a uniform arm is "wasted" toward the tip, where the bending moment is small. Removing material there softens the whole arm (more deflection per unit strain) while the root — the critical section — keeps its full depth. The thickness taper also spreads strain more evenly along the arm instead of concentrating it at the root.

4 · Assembly & removal force

During assembly the mating edge pushes on the entry ramp. Resolving the normal force N and the friction force μN along and across the insertion direction:

W=N(sin⁢α+ μ⁢cos⁢α) P=N(cos⁢α− μ⁢sin⁢α)

Dividing the two eliminates N:

W=P⁢ μ+tan⁢α 1−μ⁢tan⁢α
same equation with α′ for removal · denominator ≤ 0 → self-locking

The denominator is a real physical limit, not a math quirk: when μ·tanα ≥ 1 the harder you push, the harder the wedge grips — the idealized model predicts no finite force assembles the joint. The calculator refuses to display a number there. Used deliberately on the return face (large α′, or 90°), self-locking is how you design a permanent joint.

5 · Secant modulus

Plastics don't have one stiffness. The stress–strain curve bends over well before yield, so the tangent at zero (the datasheet "Young's modulus") overestimates stiffness at snap-fit strains of several percent. The honest single number is the secant modulus — the slope of a straight line from the origin to the operating point on the curve:

Es(ε)= σ(ε)ε

Because strain is pure geometry (§2), you can compute ε first, then read Es at that strain from the material's curve — one pass, no iteration. Es depends on temperature, moisture (nylons especially: PA66 drops from ~2800 MPa dry to ~1200 MPa conditioned) and strain rate. Use the curve for your actual grade and state.

6 · The verdict: permissible strain & utilization

A metal flexure is checked against yield. A molded snap is checked against a permissible strain εallow — a design value from the supplier, typically about half the strain at yield or break for a single assembly event, lower for repeated use. The calculator reports

U= Kt⁢εcalc εallow

U is the fraction of the strain budget spent. U = 0.40 means the click uses 40% of what the material tolerates — comfortable. U = 1 is the edge. The verdict has four states, not two: pass (model applicable, U ≤ 1), fail (U > 1), indeterminate (the model itself isn't credible here — short arm, huge deflection, self-locking ramp — so no number is trustworthy), and invalid (nonsense input). A pass clears the assembly event only; creep, fatigue, and environment need their own evidence.

7 · Getting more travel — the handbook design ideas

You mentioned the handbooks achieve much higher deflection ratios with certain designs — correct, and here is the toolbox. Permissible undercut for a given arm is ymax = εallow·L²/(Cε·t·Kt), so anything that raises L, lowers Cε, lowers Kt or shares the deflection multiplies your travel:

Design ideaGain in permissible deflectionCost / caveat
Thickness taper t → t/2×1.63 Slightly weaker hook base; supported by this calculator.
Width taper b → b/4×1.28 Narrow tip needs room for the hook; supported by this calculator.
Lengthen the arm ×1.5×2.25 (L²) Package space; retention stiffness falls with L³ — check holding force.
Both parts flexible — arm and mating edge each deflect up to ×2 The required y splits between the two members in proportion to their compliances. Needs both parts designed for it.
U-shaped / folded arm — the arm doubles back on itself ×4+ (effective L doubled in the same envelope) The big one for tight packages. Curved-root behavior is beyond this v1 beam model — design by handbook chart or FEA. Planned for v2.
Generous root fillet R/t ≥ 0.5 removes the Kt penalty (a sharp corner can cost 2–3×) Very large R creates a thick section → sink marks and voids. R/t ≈ 0.5–0.6 is the sweet spot.
Material state — e.g. conditioned PA66 instead of dry ×1.5 on εallow (4% → 6%) Only if the part really lives conditioned; dry-as-molded parts must survive assembly dry.
Assembly aids — lead-ins, shallow α, lubricant lowers W, not strain Helps force problems, not survivability. Lubricants change μ permanently — retest removal.

These multiply: a thickness-tapered arm, 1.5× longer, with a proper fillet on a conditioned nylon can legitimately take 5–8× the undercut of a naive short, sharp-cornered, uniform arm — which is exactly the spread you see between the conservative worked examples and the aggressive production designs in the manuals.

8 · A note on printed plastics

The calculator now includes educational entries for printed materials — PC-ABS (molded blend), PA12 MJF, and FDM PETG / PLA / ABS. Two honest cautions:

  • Anisotropy. FDM parts are far weaker across layer lines than along them. The beam model assumes one material in every direction — orient the arm so bending stays in the XY plane, and treat the numbers as in-plane values. MJF is much closer to isotropic but still deserves printed-coupon verification.
  • Creep and brittleness. PLA is stiff but brittle and creeps at room temperature — poor for reusable snaps. PETG and PA12 behave much better. For any printed snap that stays loaded, test a printed coupon over time; the datasheet won't tell you.
All material values in this tool are generic educational defaults, never production allowables. The material card names the state (dry / conditioned / XY printed) because the same polymer can differ by 2× between states.
