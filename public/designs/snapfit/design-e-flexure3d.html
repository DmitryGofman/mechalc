Theory & Equations
Es secant modulus · I second moment of area · L arm length · t thickness (bending direction) · b width · y undercut (tip deflection) · ε root surface strain · εallow permissible strain · U utilization · α entry ramp · α′ return ramp · μ friction

Model. A molded snap arm is a compliant mechanism: the hook rides the mating edge and the arm bends like an end-loaded Euler–Bernoulli cantilever, rigidly built in at the wall. Bending moment grows from zero at the hook to a maximum at the root, so strain peaks where the arm meets the wall — exactly where snap arms crack. The 3D arm colors this directly: the stretched face warms toward red, the compressed face cools toward blue, and the neutral axis stays a calm green, all most intense at the root. Because the snap event is displacement-controlled, the root strain ε = C·t·y/L² is pure geometry — the modulus only sets how much force the deflection takes.

Designing a snap arm. You usually fix the undercut y you need and size the arm around it. Length is the strongest lever — strain falls with 1/L² — while thinning t helps both strain (∝ t) and insertion force (∝ t³). Tapering the thickness to t/2 or the width to b/4 sheds root strain and material for the same undercut. Keep the return angle α′ below the self-locking limit unless you want a permanent joint, and treat μ as a range, not a number.

Scope. Linear small-deflection theory with a secant modulus, accurate for roughly y/L ≲ 0.1 and L/t ≳ 10; the tool warns as you leave that envelope and holds the verdict — INDETERMINATE — once the model is no longer credible (very short arms, y/L > 0.25, self-locking ramps). The 3D viewer draws a length-preserving large-deflection curve that curls further than the linear numbers imply. Creep, fatigue, weld lines and fiber orientation are not modelled; a green PASS clears the assembly event, not the product's lifetime. In short: the snap arm is a spring you design backwards — you know the travel (the undercut), and the game is spending as little strain as possible to buy it. Length is your budget; thickness is your tax.
